# Mausoleo: reading across a regime change in a digitised newspaper archive

BASC0024 Final Year Dissertation

---

## Abstract

*Il Messaggero* did not appear on 26 July 1943, the morning after the Grand Council of Fascism deposed Mussolini and the King had him arrested at Villa Savoia. The other thirty issues of the month have been digitised; the missing one has not, since there is nothing to digitise. The existing online archives that hold the paper return nothing for the date, because they index articles and the day in question has none. They are similarly mute when a user wants to know how the editorial register shifted across the regime change, or how the balance of war and domestic-politics coverage moved across the month: the user must read several hundred articles by hand and assemble the answer. This dissertation argues that a digital archive should respond to such questions structurally rather than by silence, and builds a system, Mausoleo, that does so by storing the corpus as a calendar-shaped tree of recursively summarised nodes (paragraph, article, day, week, month) over which a researcher agent navigates.

The argument is interdisciplinary. Cognitive science has documented over the past century that memory organises temporal information at multiple resolutions, and that researchers reading time-stamped material move continuously between dates, narrative episodes and longer schemas. An archival interface that flattens its corpus into a ranked article list asks the researcher to do all of that integration in their head; one that exposes the multi-resolution structure aligns with what they are already doing. Three case studies on *Il Messaggero* July 1943 (the absent 26 July, the regime change of 25 to 27 July, and the per-week balance of war and domestic-politics coverage) bear out the prediction across episodic, narrative and aggregate questions. Across eighteen scored trials the system averaged 11.3 tool calls against the keyword baseline's 28.3 and was preferred by both judges on a three-dimension rubric in every case. The missing-day case is the central one. A calendar-shaped index can hold a node for a date that contains no articles, and a summary that treats the absence as evidence; a flat index can hold neither.

## Preface

I came to this dissertation through a question about an absent newspaper. In researching the fall of Italian fascism for an undergraduate module on twentieth-century European history, I went looking for what *Il Messaggero* had reported on 26 July 1943, the morning after Mussolini's deposition. The digital fund did not have it, and the existing search interface did not tell me whether the issue had once existed and been lost, never been printed, or simply not been digitised yet. Reading around the gap meant reading the 25th and the 27th and inferring; the interface treated my question as a query that returned nothing, when in fact the absent issue is one of the most articulate documents the corpus contains.

The discipline pair this dissertation works within is cognitive science and computer science, which is the natural pairing given my BASc concentrations. From the cognitive-science side I take the substantive claim that memory is hierarchically organised across temporal resolutions and that researchers offload some of this organisation to the materials and instruments they read with. From the computer-science side I take the lineage of hierarchical retrieval (recursive-summarisation trees, graph-community summaries, table-of-contents-driven retrieval) within which the system sits, and the methodology by which it is evaluated. Both disciplines work over a corpus and its source-critical context, an Italian regime-aligned daily under wartime censorship.

Reading Bartlett alongside Hutchins and Eichenbaum while drafting the index changed how I held the engineering problem. I had set out to build a fast retriever for a noisy corpus; what the cognitive material shifted was the question, towards how a retriever might externalise the structure historians were already imposing on time-stamped material. The architecture in the chapters below follows from that shift.

I came to the project from a background in machine learning, with two undergraduate modules in historical methodology and a working knowledge of Italian. The first six weeks went on the OCR ensemble (eight sub-pipelines on two GPUs, a hand-cleaned 1885 evaluation issue I retranscribed twice because my first pass missed the column-six fiction header) before the index design was even drafted. I thank Dr Yi Gong for supervising; her comments on the cognitive-science framing and on what the system can and cannot warrant shaped the chapters that follow.

## Chapter 1: A missing newspaper

Late on the evening of 24 July 1943, the Grand Council of Fascism convened in Palazzo Venezia and at 02:40 the next morning voted nineteen to seven to restore constitutional powers to the King. By the afternoon of 25 July, Mussolini had been received at Villa Savoia, dismissed, and arrested as he left the building; by midnight, Marshal Pietro Badoglio was prime minister. The 25 July morning edition of *Il Messaggero*, Rome's principal daily and a regime-aligned title under the directives of the *MinCulPop*, went to press long before any of this happened and in its accustomed late-fascist register. The 26 July edition does not appear in the digitised fund. The 27 July edition reappeared under the new government, with the Grand Council *ordine del giorno*, the King's proclamation, and the new ministry on the front page.

In any of the existing digitised newspaper archives that hold *Il Messaggero* (Chronicling America, Europeana Newspapers, Impresso, with their counterparts in the Italian *Emeroteca digitale*), a query for what the paper printed on 26 July returns nothing. The archives index articles, the missing day has none, and the indexer has no representation for the day in itself. Historians have always read around such gaps, taking the 25th and the 27th and inferring backwards from what survives; what the interface cannot do is register that the absent day is part of the corpus's testimony, the editorial silence of those twenty-four hours of regime transition. The narrower question of how the paper's editorial register shifted across the three days of the deposition similarly leaves the user with thirty articles to read and a tonal trajectory to reconstruct by hand. The wider question of how the war and domestic-politics balance moved across the month leaves the user with several hundred articles, a classification scheme to construct, and a monthly aggregate to compose.

The cognitive-science literature on memory has been suggesting for the better part of a century that the kind of multi-resolution movement involved here, between a date-bound episode, a few-day narrative, and a month-scale shape, is constitutive of how researchers read time-stamped material rather than incidental to it. The 1930s work on schema demonstrated that what survives recall is a compressed template rather than the verbatim event (Bartlett, 1932). Working-memory research from the mid-century onwards then fixed the active budget at a small handful of items at any moment (Miller, 1956; Cowan, 2001), which means that any month-scale question must be answered through compression at some level. A separate strand, running from Tolman's spatial cognitive maps through Eichenbaum's integrative account of the hippocampus and the more recent computational models of relational learning, suggests that the hierarchical-relational machinery the brain uses for navigating space is recruited for navigating time and concept (Tolman, 1948; Eichenbaum, 2017; Whittington et al., 2020). When a researcher reads an archive at multiple resolutions, the interface either holds the resolutions or asks the researcher to hold them. A flat keyword search holds none.

This dissertation builds and tests an interface that does hold them. Mausoleo stores the *Il Messaggero* July 1943 corpus (thirty surviving issues, around 6,480 hand-cleaned article transcriptions) as a calendar-shaped tree of recursively summarised nodes at five levels (paragraph, article, day, week, month). The day node for 26 July exists, with empty leaves and a summary that contextualises the absence; the week-of-25-July node holds an integrated summary of the regime-change trajectory; the month node holds the schematic monthly shape against which the per-week war/domestic balance can be read. A researcher agent navigates the tree and may also issue keyword and embedding searches over the same corpus. Three case studies on the July 1943 material, evaluated against a keyword baseline over identical article transcriptions, ask whether the multi-resolution interface improves on the flat one across the question types above.

The system sits in a recent lineage of hierarchical-retrieval methods in natural-language processing that build trees of summaries over corpora, by recursive clustering of chunk embeddings, by community detection over an entity-relation graph, or by exposing a document's table of contents. Mausoleo's contribution is the choice not to induce: the hierarchy is the corpus's own publication calendar. That choice is what makes the absent-day node representable. A calendar has a slot for 26 July whether or not anything was printed on it; an induced hierarchy clusters what exists and so cannot represent what does not.

The remaining chapters develop the argument. Chapter two sets the system against existing digitised newspaper archives and the hierarchical-retrieval lineage, develops the cognitive-science material that motivates the design, and gives the source-critical context of the corpus. Chapter three describes how Mausoleo is built. Chapter four reports the case studies, with the absent 26 July developed at length and the regime-change and comparative-coverage cases as briefer contrasts. Chapter five discusses what the cases show and what they leave open, and chapter six closes by returning to the absent day and to its wider implications for how a digital archive holds a missing source.

## Chapter 2: Three literatures and a corpus

Mausoleo sits at an intersection that three mature literatures address only obliquely. The existing digital newspaper archives are infrastructurally indispensable but mostly query-driven; the hierarchical-retrieval lineage in information retrieval shows that multi-resolution access is tractable but usually induces its hierarchies from data; the cognitive-science material on memory and external cognition has been arguing for half a century that researchers organise temporal information hierarchically without that argument being operationalised in interface design. The corpus has its own historiography, which determines what the case studies can mean.

### Existing digitised newspaper archives

Three systems define the field. *Chronicling America*, the National Digital Newspaper Program at the Library of Congress, holds roughly 23 million pages of US newsprint dating from 1690 to 1963 and exposes them through faceted search over OCR'd full text. *Europeana Newspapers* aggregates around 28 million pages across forty European languages, again through full-text search backed by OCR of variable quality and limited named-entity enrichment. *Impresso*, the Swiss and Luxembourgish project that is the closest comparator to any new venture in this area, covers roughly two centuries of French, German and Luxembourgish newspapers and sits at the enriched end of the spectrum: alongside OCR it offers named-entity recognition, topic models, lexical-semantic comparison and text-reuse browsing (Ehrmann et al., 2020; Düring et al., 2024).

What unites these systems is the access template. The user arrives with a query, receives a ranked article list, optionally facets the result by date, year or source, and then reads articles individually. Düring et al. (2024) describe the Impresso interface as one of "transparent generosity" about exposing intermediate structure, but the user is still expected to come with a search term in mind. The presupposition is reasonable for most historical research, where the historian arrives with a question already framed. It is less reasonable for the historian who wants to understand a corpus they cannot read in full at the article level, or for whom the answer to the question is not a list of articles but a shape across days, or an absence.

### The hierarchical-retrieval lineage

Classical information retrieval supplies the baseline. Salton, Wong and Yang (1975) introduced the vector space model; Robertson and Zaragoza (2009) consolidated the probabilistic-relevance tradition that culminated in BM25, still the strongest sparse baseline. Both treat the document collection as a flat set: relevance is computed per document and the structure of the collection plays no role in retrieval.

The recent hierarchical-retrieval lineage breaks with this assumption. RAPTOR (Sarthi et al., 2024) recursively clusters and summarises chunk embeddings bottom-up to induce a tree, then retrieves at multiple abstraction levels, allowing a query to hit a high-level summary or a leaf chunk depending on its scope. GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) extracts an entity-relation graph from a corpus, runs Leiden community detection over it, and produces hierarchical community summaries that the system can retrieve at a chosen level of abstraction; the authors report substantial gains on comprehensiveness and diversity over flat retrieval-augmented generation for global-summarisation queries. PageIndex (Zhang and Tang, 2025) takes a third route, exposing a document's table-of-contents structure as the retrieval substrate so that the system can navigate by section rather than by chunk. Across the three, the common move is to give the retriever something more than a flat list to work with; they differ in whether the hierarchy is induced by clustering, by graph-community detection or by surface document structure.

The closest prior work on retrieval-augmented generation over historical newspapers is Murugaraj et al. (2025), who apply a topic-restricted retrieval pipeline to the Impresso Swiss corpus and report improved retrieval relevance over flat retrieval-augmented generation, measured by BERTScore, ROUGE and UniEval. Their pipeline restricts retrieval to documents matching the query's inferred topic, on the assumption that topical coherence between query and retrieved articles is the dominant signal of relevance. That assumption is hard to defend on a corpus with a strong native temporal hierarchy. For a question about a regime collapse, the topically most-relevant articles cluster around the surface vocabulary of fascism (Mussolini, Grand Council, Badoglio), and a topic-restricted retriever returns them at a uniform editorial register, flattening the shift the question asks about. The metrics used in their evaluation reward semantic overlap between answer and reference and do not measure whether the answer captures a temporal trajectory. Their question and Mausoleo's are different: theirs is, given a query, retrieve the topically relevant articles; Mausoleo's is, given a temporal slice, return what the slice looked like, including its absences. A direct head-to-head on Impresso would be the obvious next step, but the corpus is in French and German and the case-study agent and judges in chapter four are configured for Italian.

Mausoleo extends this lineage in one specific respect. The prior systems above apply hierarchical retrieval either to a single document's section structure or to a clustering induced from chunk embeddings; Mausoleo applies the same paradigm to an archival corpus whose hierarchy is given by chronology rather than induced from the data. This is methodologically defensible because daily newspapers already carry a native temporal hierarchy, and constructing the index over the calendar preserves provenance by construction.

### Memory, hierarchy and external structure

Why the calendar-given hierarchy should be the right shape of index for an archival interface, rather than one designer's preference, is the substantive cognitive-science claim the dissertation rests on. Four strands from the literature support it.

Memory is hierarchically organised. Bartlett (1932) introduced the term *schema* for the compressed, generic templates that organise specific recollections, and showed in his *War of the Ghosts* studies that what survives recall over time is the schema rather than the verbatim event. Craik and Lockhart (1972) reframed retention as a function of depth of processing, with deeper semantic encoding producing more durable traces. The contemporary picture distinguishes episodic memory, events bound to time and place, from semantic memory, compressed general knowledge, and from the schema-level abstractions Bartlett described, with episodic traces consolidating over time into semantic and schematic structures. The relevance to an archival interface is direct. A corpus contains material at every level (specific articles, narrative episodes across days, monthly aggregates), and a researcher reading the corpus moves between those levels.

Working memory imposes a tight chunking constraint. Miller's (1956) *magical number seven plus or minus two* and Cowan's (2001) revised estimate of about four functional chunks together establish that the active workspace cannot hold more than a small handful of items at any moment. The implication for an external retrieval system is that it must compress at multiple resolutions to be cognitively tractable. Thirty article snippets exceed active capacity; one day-summary at the right level of abstraction sits comfortably within it.

The same hierarchical-relational organisation extends from space to time and concept. Tolman (1948) showed that rats build *cognitive maps* exceeding stimulus-response chains. Eichenbaum (2017) consolidated the view that the hippocampus encodes spatial position, temporal sequence and conceptual relation in a shared representational format. Behrens et al. (2018) reviewed the evidence that the spatial-coding machinery is recruited for non-spatial structural inference, and Whittington et al. (2020), in the Tolman-Eichenbaum-Machine, modelled the same circuit as a general-purpose relational learner factorising structure from content. The substantive lesson is that the hierarchical-relational machinery the brain uses for space is the same it uses for time and concept; a multi-resolution temporal index is therefore an instantiation of the same structural form, not a metaphor on cognition.

Cognition routinely offloads to external structures. Hutchins (1995), in *Cognition in the Wild*, showed that real cognitive work is distributed across people, instruments and inscriptions, with each component carrying a piece of the computation. Clark and Chalmers (1998) made the philosophical case for the *extended mind*: when an external resource is reliably and habitually consulted, it functions as part of the cognitive system. An archive interface, on this view, is part of the cognitive system the historian uses; its structural choices matter cognitively, not just ergonomically. An interface that exposes multi-resolution temporal structure aligns with the cognitive architecture researchers bring to historical material; one that flattens it asks the researcher to do all the chunking, narrative integration and schema-formation in their head, against cognitive evidence that they are already representing the material that way. The case studies in chapter four ask whether this prediction holds in measurable form.

### The corpus and its source-critical context

The cognitive framing motivates the design; the corpus context determines what the case studies can mean historically. *Il Messaggero* in July 1943 was a regime-aligned daily under the directives of the *MinCulPop*. Schudson (1978) treats the social construction of news as itself a historical process; Murialdi (1986) remains the standard treatment of the Italian press across the regime period and documents the directive system that shaped what could and could not appear in print. The principal argument of the dissertation is the cognitive one developed above; the corpus context is what makes the case-study questions meaningful as questions a working historian would put to the material. Any summary the index produces is a derived secondary source whose relation to the regime-aligned primary text must remain auditable: the schema's separation of leaf text from summary text means the original paragraphs remain reachable from every higher-level node. The commitment is the one ISAD(G) names as *respect des fonds* and original order (International Council on Archives, 2000); the catalogue must not rearrange the source.

## Chapter 3: How Mausoleo is built

Mausoleo is three loosely coupled stages connected by a single ClickHouse table called `nodes`: an OCR pipeline that produces hand-cleanable article-level transcriptions from page scans; a recursive summariser that builds the calendar-shaped tree over those transcriptions; and a small JSON-emitting command-line interface through which a researcher agent reads the tree. The boundary between stages is the schema of the table, so each stage can be swapped or replayed without touching the others.

### From scanned pages to article transcriptions

The input is a directory of scanned JPEGs, six pages per issue on average, for the thirty surviving July 1943 issues of *Il Messaggero*. The output is a per-issue JSON file listing detected articles with headline, body text and page span. The pipeline runs cold-cache, regenerating every prediction from the raw images on each invocation, under a budget of thirty minutes wall-clock per issue on two GPUs.

Eight sub-pipelines run in parallel across the two GPUs, and their predictions are merged deterministically. Cross-family diversity is deliberate: three model configurations (an open-weight vision-language model in two backbone versions, the second under a strict generation mode) load on each GPU, and the eight sub-pipelines vary column-split granularity (full-page, two- through six-column splits, plus a small-region detector running over layout boxes) so that no single layout assumption dominates. The Qwen2.5-VL backbone is a vision-language model trained for dense document understanding (Bai et al., 2025), used here as a black-box OCR engine prompted to emit structured article JSON. The eight per-source predictions merge through a deterministic chain (a REPLACE pass, an ADDITIVE pass for the column-six advertisements source, and a quality-weighted text selector) without any LLM arbitration or post-correction. Both LLM arbitration and prompt-based post-correction were tried during the hill-climb and rejected: the post-corrector modernises good articles into paraphrases more than it repairs bad ones, costing 0.6 to 1.1 composite points across three serious attempts.

Two empirical observations drive the design. Column-split predictions from a single model are highly correlated, so a fifth column-three pipeline of the same family contributes less than a different model family at the same column count; cross-family diversity buys roughly 0.013 composite over the best single-family ensemble at equal wall-clock. The wall-clock ceiling matters: an unconstrained research configuration reaches 0.92 composite but takes fifty to sixty minutes per issue, outside what a one-month corpus build at 30.5 min per issue can sustain.

The cold-cache composite, evaluated on two hand-cleaned issues (1885-06-15, 41 articles; 1910-06-15, 193 articles) with an article-level matching scheme, is 0.89878 averaged across the two issues, decomposing to 0.872 on 1885 and 0.926 on 1910. The composite weights length-weighted character error rate at 0.40, recall against a Jaccard matching threshold at 0.25, ordering at 0.15, headline character error rate at 0.10, and page-accuracy at 0.10. Article-level matching is preferred to flat full-text character error rate because the latter penalises any displacement of an article in reading order even when its text is character-perfect; this is the move that historical-newspaper OCR work in NewsEye (Doucet et al., 2020) helped make standard. The single biggest gain across the hill-climb was not a model addition: a post-processing filter that strips raw-JSON regurgitations that occasionally leak from the vision-language sources added 0.0165 alone. A leave-one-out at the five-source intermediate stage confirms no entry is redundant. Per-pipeline component scores and configuration tables are in Appendix A.

The 6,480 article-level transcriptions used downstream are a hand-cleaned post-pass of the ensemble's 9,456 raw articles (deduplication and cross-page stitching), outside the OCR composite score.

### The calendar-shaped tree

The index is a single ClickHouse table whose rows hold the level (paragraph, article, day, week, month, with the production schema also supporting year and decade above month and an archive root above decade), a parent identifier, a sibling position, a date range, a summary, an embedding vector, and, for paragraph leaves only, the raw OCR'd text. Higher-level nodes hold no raw text of their own; their content is the summary plus a pointer downward. This is the architectural form of Ketelaar's (2001) observation that an archival description is a tacit narrative, an activation of the source rather than a replacement for it. The summary is never authoritative; the leaves are.

Node identifiers are deterministic and human-readable: `1943-07-25_a127_p03` for a paragraph, `1943-07-25_a127` for an article, `1943-07-25` for a day, `1943-W29` for a week, `1943-07` for a month. Two secondary indexes sit on the table: a vector-similarity index over the embedding column for nearest-neighbour search, and a token-bloom-filter index over the summary column for keyword search.

Construction is bottom-up, one level at a time. Article summaries are produced by a batch over each article's paragraphs; day, week and month summaries by the same recursion at coarser grain. The lineage is the recursive book summarisation of Wu et al. (2021), which establishes that bottom-up summarisation over a fixed branching factor produces coherent abstractions without exceeding any context window. The summariser prompt is consistent across levels: target two to four hundred words, weave named entities into the prose rather than listing them, preserve dates and quantities verbatim, and write so that an agent can decide from the summary alone whether to descend further. Length is held constant deliberately, which makes navigation predictable but forces aggressive thematic compression at higher levels. A spot-check against ten July 1943 day summaries (excluding the absent 26 July) recovered forty-eight of fifty top named entities through accent-stripped substring match against the day's hand-cleaned transcription; the two misses were both `Papa Pio XII`, where the summariser inserted an editorial honorific the press itself never used, preferring `Pio XII` or `il Pontefice`. An information-loss trace on 1943-07-03 gives the compression rate precisely: of thirty-six distinct named entities in the day's three longest articles, seven survive at day level, six at week, one (`Il Messaggero` itself) at month. The pattern across entity types matches that headline rate: generic organisational acronyms drop out by week; specific persons remain visible at week but are subsumed by month; place names hold up at day but disappear at month. Month-level navigation is therefore for what kind of day it was, not for who was in it.

For July 1943, 6,480 article nodes collapse into 31 day nodes, 5 weeks, and 1 month root (6,517 nodes total). The 26 July day node exists even though its leaf paragraphs are empty: the absent issue is stored as a first-class node whose summary contextualises the absence, which is the architectural precondition for the lead case study. The week-of-25-July summary closes with an explicit prolepsis added by the summariser (*Sconosciuto ai lettori del Messaggero: il giorno successivo, 25 luglio, avverrà l'arresto di Mussolini*), which is the summariser supplying historical context the source could not contain; the next chapter returns to it as a summariser-bias phenomenon. At month level the prolepsis collapses into *l'arresto di Mussolini (25 luglio)*, with the 02:40 timestamp and the Grand Council mechanism compressed away, while the absent 26 July issue is named as documenting *le 24 ore di vuoto*.

### How the agent reads the tree

The retrieval surface is a small server backed by ClickHouse plus a typer-based command-line interface whose user is a researcher agent, so every command emits structured JSON to standard output. Tree traversal is exposed through `GET /root`, `/nodes/{id}`, `/nodes/{id}/children`, `/nodes/{id}/parent` and `/nodes/{id}/text` (raw text for leaves, reconstructed from descendants for higher nodes). Search is exposed through `POST /search/semantic` (vector approximate-nearest-neighbour over the vector-similarity index, filterable by level or date range), `/search/text` (token-bloom over summary text) and `/search/hybrid` (a weighted combination). A `GET /stats` endpoint reports per-level node counts.

The interaction pattern is closer to a ReAct loop (Yao et al., 2022) than to single-shot retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020). The agent enters at the root, reads a summary, decides whether to descend or to search, iterates. Tree traversal supplies provenance and chronological position; semantic search is the escape hatch when chronology is the wrong axis. The application programming interface is minimal and stateless; reasoning is pushed into the agent.

## Chapter 4: Three case studies

The previous chapter set out how Mausoleo is built. This chapter asks whether the design makes a measurable difference, against a flat keyword baseline, on three questions a working researcher would put to the July 1943 corpus. The first case is the missing 26 July, an episodic-retrieval task in the sense of chapter two: the answer is bound to a specific date. The second is the regime change of 25 to 27 July, a narrative-integration task: the answer is the trajectory across episodes. The third is the per-week balance of war and domestic-politics coverage, an aggregate or schema-formation task: the answer is the compressed monthly shape, the kind of representation Bartlett called a schema. The three together cover the multi-resolution span the cognitive framing predicts. The first case is reported at length because it sets the architectural commitments most clearly; the second and third are reported more briefly, with the aggregate table at the end gathering all eighteen trials.

### Setup

Both systems share the corpus, the researcher agent, the task wording and the interaction budget; only the toolset differs. The baseline is BM25 over the same hand-cleaned article transcriptions in the `documents` table, ignoring the `nodes` hierarchy. Mausoleo is the agent-mediated tree traversal of the previous chapter, with the same `documents` corpus plus the `nodes` table and the semantic, text and hybrid search endpoints. The researcher agent is held constant in both arms (a contemporary commercial LLM under identical system prompt), with a tool-call cap of thirty per trial and three trials per cell at distinct seed prompts. Holding the agent constant and varying only the tools forestalls the concern that any difference observed is the agent's win rather than the index's. Three metrics are applied uniformly: tool calls and characters returned to the agent's context, for efficiency; recall against a hand-built relevance ground truth for the first two cases and ratio mean absolute error and root-mean-square error against a per-week war/domestic oracle for the third, for completeness; and the mean of two LLM judges scoring a three-dimension rubric (factual accuracy, comprehensiveness, insight) at zero to five per dimension, for quality. The two judges share a vendor and are distinguished only by system prompt; cross-vendor robustness is untested. Inter-judge κ is reported per case, and a paired sign test across the three trials by two judges per metric. The relevance ground truth for the first two cases was hand-annotated against four works of historiography (Pavone, 1991; Murialdi, 1986; Bosworth, 2005; Deakin, 1962); single-annotator construction is a limitation.

### The missing 26 July (lead case)

What did *Il Messaggero* report on 26 July 1943, the day after the deposition of Mussolini? The single most consequential fact about this date in the digitised corpus is that the issue is not there. A date-bounded query against the article corpus returns the empty set. The case leads because it is the definitional capability gap of the indexing architecture for an episodic task: a flat retrieval index has no node for a missing date, so the retriever can only return the empty set and pass the absence to the agent as a null. A calendar-shaped tree instantiates the day node `1943-07-26` regardless of whether its leaves contain text, and attaches a summary that contextualises the gap. The episodic-memory analogue runs in the same direction: an event whose temporal slot the memory system does not hold cannot register as missing at all, only fail to come back when looked for.

The baseline cannot surface the absence as a node. A keyword query for a date with zero hits returns nothing the agent can interpret directly; the researcher agent issues 27.0 tool calls on average (range 26 to 28), reading the 25 and 27 July issues that bracket the gap and inferring backwards. The compiled answer scores recall of 0.67 (range 0.62 to 0.69) against the 42-article relevance set and judge-mean quality of 4.22 (range 4.00 to 5.00). In two of three trials the baseline agent infers the absence without ever issuing a query for 26 July material, reasoning around the data deficit from its training-corpus knowledge of the regime change. The interface treats a question that has an answer as if it had no answer; the agent then supplies one from outside the source.

Mausoleo can ground the question. The day node `1943-07-26` exists in the `nodes` table even though its leaf paragraphs are empty, and its summary, generated at index-build time, is the answer to the question:

> [edizione assente: il fondo archivistico digitalizzato non contiene il numero del 26 luglio 1943 de «Il Messaggero». Il giorno precedente, notte fra 24 e 25 luglio, il Gran Consiglio del Fascismo aveva votato l'ordine del giorno Grandi alle 02:40, e nel pomeriggio del 25 Vittorio Emanuele III aveva fatto arrestare Mussolini all'uscita da Villa Savoia. Il giornale del 27 luglio riapparirà sotto il nuovo governo Badoglio. La lacuna archivistica del 26 luglio è essa stessa documento: testimonia il vuoto editoriale di quelle 24 ore di transizione di regime.]

The Mausoleo agent reaches this node in 13.3 tool calls on average (range 11 to 15), and its compiled answers score a judge mean of 4.56 (range 4.00 to 5.00) against the baseline's 4.22. Recall against the article-id ground truth is 0.67, tied at the mean with the baseline; the dispersion across trials is diagnosed in the variance note in the supplementary material. The judges disagree at κ = 0.33; the paired sign test on quality (n = 6) returns p = 0.625, so the case is carried by what the architecture allows rather than by the statistical resolution of the experiment. The recall tie at 0.67 is itself the symptom: touched-articles is the wrong instrument when the answer is an absent issue.

The architecture is what does the work here. Date sits as a first-class structural property of the index, an absent date instantiates a first-class node, that node carries a summary, and the absence becomes addressable from within the index rather than from outside it. A flat retriever would need ad-hoc out-of-band metadata to recover the same affordance: a manually maintained list of known-missing issues, a separate gap-aware presentation layer, or an agent prompt instructing the system to check for absences. The calendar-given hierarchy obviates the workaround by making the gap part of the index by construction.

A wider observation. The interface that returns the empty set for 26 July is not failing to retrieve an article; it is failing to hold a representation of a date. Romans reading the paper that morning read the news of the deposition before the morning paper would normally have arrived, and noticed that there was no morning paper to read; the absence was part of the experience of the day, in the way Pavone (1991) treats the *interregno* between deposition and 8 September as a category of historical experience rather than a vacancy in the record. Treating the absence as a missing query result rather than as documentation is what the indexing architecture has to refuse. In Mausoleo's three trials the agent reaches `1943-07-26`, reads the summary, and incorporates the absence as evidence into its compiled answer; in two of the baseline's three trials the agent never asks the index whether the 26 July edition exists, taking its own background knowledge as the source. The compiled answers therefore differ in a way the touched-set recall metric does not see: one is anchored to the index's testimony, the other supplied from beyond it.

### The 25 July regime change

How did *Il Messaggero* cover the fall of Mussolini and the transition to Badoglio? The 33-article relevance ground truth spans three days: the 25 July morning issue, printed before the Grand Council vote and so in late-fascist register (*DOVE ARRIVANO I LIBERATORI* sarcasm, *BIECO FUREORE BRITANNICO* (OCR sic, *Il Messaggero* 25-Jul-1943)); the absent 26 July; and the 27 July reappearance under Badoglio with the new ministry, the Grand Council *ordine del giorno*, and the King's proclamation. The case is a narrative reconstruction across an editorial register shift, which the day-summary nodes are designed to support: each day summary characterises editorial tone alongside content, so the shift is legible from two summary reads rather than a fan-out across hundreds of snippets.

Mausoleo uses 12.3 tool calls on average (range 11 to 13); the baseline uses 29.7 (range 29 to 30), saturating the budget in every trial. Recall against the ground truth is 0.76 for Mausoleo (zero variance) and 0.62 for the baseline (range 0.55 to 0.70). Quality is 4.83 (range 4.67 to 5.00) for Mausoleo and 4.44 (range 4.00 to 5.00) for the baseline. Inter-judge agreement is the highest of the three cases (κ = 0.57). The paired sign test gives p = 0.250 on completeness and p = 0.375 on quality. Mausoleo descends from the month root to the days of 25 and 27 July, reads the day summaries, and identifies the register shift directly from the prose; the baseline issues keyword queries for *Mussolini*, *Badoglio*, *Gran Consiglio* and *Re Vittorio Emanuele*, reads the ranked article list and compiles a narrative, capturing the political event but tending to miss the editorial-tone shift unless the agent reads in chronological order, which keyword ranking does not enforce.

### The per-week balance across the month

How did the balance of war coverage and domestic-politics coverage shift across July 1943? An aggregate or schema-formation question of this kind is where the hierarchy is supposed to pay off. Each day-summary node already characterises the day's editorial balance, so the agent aggregates across thirty-one summaries rather than thousands of articles. The first-run metric was article-id recall against a 27-article set, which produced a misleading 0.07 for Mausoleo against 0.11 for the baseline: day summaries do not enumerate article identifiers, so an aggregate-question system that answers via compressed summaries collapses to near-zero on touched-set recall by construction. For the rerun, all 6,480 July 1943 articles were classified by an LLM (batched ten per call, temperature 0) into WAR / DOMESTIC / OTHER and reduced to a per-week war fraction. Oracle: W26 0.558, W27 0.589, W28 0.620, W29 0.733, W30 0.416. Both systems emit five `WEEK 1943-WNN: war_fraction=<float>` lines, scored on mean absolute error and root-mean-square error against the oracle. Mausoleo wins on ratio accuracy (mean absolute error 0.149 vs 0.194, root-mean-square error 0.166 vs 0.220), on tool calls (8.3 vs 28.3), and on judge-mean quality (4.06 vs 3.17, the largest quality margin of the three cases). Inter-judge κ is the lowest at 0.14, reflecting the difficulty both judges have scoring an aggregate-shape answer against a rubric designed for narrative completeness. The paired sign test gives p = 0.250 on ratio root-mean-square error and p = 0.125 on quality. Once the metric matches the question, the structural prediction holds.

## Chapter 5: What the cases show

Mausoleo wins consistently across the three cases, but the shape of the win varies with the question, and the variation is what the cognitive framing predicts. The aggregate question is answered cheaply by reading day summaries (in chapter four's case three Mausoleo descends to the relevant nodes, reads two or three summaries and assembles its answer, where the baseline reconstructs the same picture article by article); the cost difference is starkest there. The narrative question is answered through a small chain of day summaries, which is the level at which the editorial-tone shift is legible; Mausoleo's twelve calls against the baseline's saturated thirty is the clearest efficiency gap. The episodic question is answered through an absent-day node, which the baseline cannot represent at all; the recall tie at 0.67 is misleading, because the baseline's answer is a reconstruction from sibling-day evidence and from the agent's training-corpus knowledge of the regime change, while Mausoleo's answer is anchored to the index's representation of the absent date.

The pattern lines up with the cognitive-science material in chapter two. The chunking constraint of working memory (Miller, 1956; Cowan, 2001) explains the shape of the efficiency gap. The baseline saturates its tool-call budget reading article-level material because that is the granularity its index exposes; Mausoleo finishes in roughly half the calls reading summaries that fit into the active workspace, so the gap is the chunking constraint expressed as a tool-call count. The three cases falling along a gradient from episodic to narrative to schematic is what Bartlett (1932) and the consolidation literature would lead one to expect a researcher to be doing when reading a month of an archive. The hippocampal machinery that organises spatial navigation is the same that organises temporal sequence and conceptual relation (Eichenbaum, 2017; Whittington et al., 2020); a multi-resolution temporal index is therefore the same structural form externally, not a metaphor on cognition. The extended-mind argument (Clark and Chalmers, 1998) licenses treating the index as part of the cognitive system: when a researcher habitually descends through Mausoleo, the index functions as part of the system the researcher uses, and the schema's separation of leaf text and summary text means the chunking work can be offloaded to the index without losing access to the source.

The architecture is not free, and several second-order features should be flagged before the closing argument. The one-month corpus took roughly thirty GPU-hours on two consumer GPUs for OCR, with a modest computational cost for LLM summarisation and an end-to-end wall of about two and a quarter hours above OCR; OCR cost extrapolates linearly to a sixty-year run, while the higher index tiers (week, month, year, decade) collapse upward and scale sub-linearly. The summariser does interpretive activation as well as compression: the week-of-25-July prolepsis on Mussolini's still-unannounced arrest is summariser activation rather than source content, and the bias-quantification step (human-written reference summaries at day, week and month for July 1943 alone) is a separate undertaking of weeks. The schema preserves the source layer beneath, but the activation layer does more interpretive work than this dissertation can audit. The evaluation runs on a single corpus and a single politically-volatile month; less volatile corpora may not behave the same way. Both judges share a vendor and are distinguished only by system prompt; cross-vendor robustness is untested. The three-dimension rubric was designed for narrative completeness and fits the comparative-coverage answer poorly (κ = 0.14). Relevance ground truth is single-annotator; second-annotator inter-rater agreement is the obvious next step. Three trials per case make the paired sign test appropriate but do not bound effect-size estimation. The architecture assumes a strong native temporal hierarchy in the source; archives without dated issues sit outside its scope.

A scope caveat carries weight. The case studies in chapter four substitute an LLM agent for the cognitive process that chapter two describes. What they directly support is the technical claim, that calendar-given hierarchies admit absent-date nodes that learned hierarchies cannot, and that an interface organised at the resolutions at which the question is posed outperforms one that flattens them. The cognitive claim, that the architecture works through alignment with multi-resolution temporal memory, would need a human-subjects study with working historians to test against the cognitive process directly rather than its computational proxy.

## Chapter 6: An archive that holds an absence

The dissertation began with a researcher walking up to a digital archive and asking what *Il Messaggero* had printed on 26 July 1943. The archive returned the empty set. The work since then has been to build an interface that does not return the empty set when the date is missing, and to argue that the design choice (storing the corpus as a calendar-shaped tree rather than a flat article list) is what makes the difference. The case studies bear out the prediction across three question types. What remains is to say what changes about how to think about archives once an interface like this is on the table.

The archives the field has built so far are extraordinary as digitisation projects and underdeveloped as cognitive instruments. They store articles, expose them through keyword search, and treat the act of reading the corpus as the user's problem. The user is implicitly assumed to arrive with a query and to leave with a list of articles, as if the historian's question were always answerable in that form and as if the structure of the corpus were of no consequence to the question. What the missing 26 July shows is that the assumption is wrong even at the simplest level: a question about a specific date is not always answerable as a list of articles, because the date may not have any articles, and the gap belongs to the source rather than to the query. Treating the source's structure as part of what the interface holds, rather than as scaffolding the interface ignores, is the change the system argues for.

The interdisciplinary claim is that a digital archive is part of the cognitive system the historian uses, and that what the cognitive-science literature has documented about how memory organises temporal information across resolutions is therefore directly relevant to how the interface should be built. The claim is not that the system reproduces a memory architecture (it does not, and the case-study evaluation runs against an LLM agent rather than human researchers), but that the structural form an interface takes matters cognitively because the interface is part of the system the researcher reads with. The calendar-shaped tree is one design that takes the form seriously. There will be others; the wider point is that the choice is a substantive one and that flat keyword search makes it by default in the wrong direction.

What the absence on 26 July records, in the end, is what Romans woke up to that morning: a regime collapsed overnight and a paper that had not arrived. The calendar-shaped index does not produce this fact, which has always been visible to anyone working through the corpus by hand. What it does is reserve a place for the date in the catalogue, with a note explaining what the gap is, so that historians using the archive at scale read the silence as part of the source rather than past it.
