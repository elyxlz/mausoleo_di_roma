# Historiography: Multi-Scale Time, Microhistory, Newspapers as Sources

## Why this matters for Mausoleo

Mausoleo's seven-level temporal hierarchy (Paragraph -> Article -> Day -> Month -> Year -> Decade -> Archive) is not a neutral data structure: it is a historiographic instrument. Each level corresponds to a register of historical analysis with its own canonical literature. Paragraph/Article is the territory of *histoire événementielle* and microhistory (Ginzburg, Levi); Day/Month/Year corresponds to Braudel's *conjoncture*; Decade/Archive approaches the *longue durée*. The dissertation's central methodological claim - that one interface enables both event-level and structural-level reading - directly answers Braudel's challenge of linking different "structures of historical time."

Treating *Il Messaggero* as a newspaper-archive forces engagement with two literatures simultaneously: press historiography, which insists newspapers are ideologically inflected, agenda-setting artefacts (especially under fascism); and digital/computational history, which has only recently produced robust precedents (impresso, Blaxill) for the kind of hierarchical indexing Mausoleo proposes.

The choice of one month in 1943 - likely straddling or adjacent to the 25 July *crollo del fascismo* - means the corpus encodes a regime change in real time. Memory studies (Nora, Assmann) frame why this slice of newsprint is itself a *lieu de mémoire*: the daily paper is the rough draft of the cultural memory politicised in postwar Italy.

## Foundational references

### 1. Annales school / multi-scale time
- **Bloch, Marc.** *Apologie pour l'histoire ou métier d'historien* (1949 / Eng. *The Historian's Craft*, 1953). Foundational defence of history as critical engagement with traces; theory of the *document* as evidence requiring interrogation. Direct relevance: Mausoleo's hierarchy is a system of structured "traces."
- **Febvre, Lucien.** *Combats pour l'histoire* (1953). Polemic for *histoire-problème* and against narrow event-history; early call for serial sources (the press is paradigmatic).
- **Braudel, Fernand.** "Histoire et sciences sociales: la longue durée" (*Annales ESC*, 1958) and *La Méditerranée et le monde méditerranéen à l'époque de Philippe II* (1949). The canonical three-tier scheme: *événement / conjoncture / longue durée*. Mausoleo's hierarchy is a digital re-instantiation of this scheme.

### 2. Microhistory
- **Ginzburg, Carlo.** *Il formaggio e i vermi* (1976; *The Cheese and the Worms*, 1980). Method of "evidential paradigm": reading anomalous archival fragments for traces of mentalité. Mausoleo's paragraph-level granularity is the technical correlate of Ginzburg's close-reading practice (MIT Press Reader, "Carlo Ginzburg Launches Microhistory").
- **Levi, Giovanni.** "On Microhistory," in P. Burke (ed.), *New Perspectives on Historical Writing* (1991). Theorises scale-reduction as epistemic move, not topical choice.
- **Ginzburg, Carlo.** "Clues: Roots of an Evidential Paradigm" (in *Myths, Emblems, Clues*, 1989). Defends inferential reading from indices - parallels keyword/entity-based retrieval.

### 3. Distant reading / quantitative literary history
- **Moretti, Franco.** "Conjectures on World Literature" (*New Left Review*, 2000); *Distant Reading* (Verso, 2013). Coined "distant reading"; argued archival-scale corpora demand non-close-reading methods (Wikipedia, "Distant reading"; LARB symposium).
- **Moretti, Franco.** *Graphs, Maps, Trees* (2005). Visual/quantitative formalism applicable to newspaper content distributions.

### 4. Public history, memory, archives
- **Nora, Pierre (ed.).** *Les Lieux de mémoire* (3 vols, 1984-1992); "Between Memory and History: Les Lieux de Mémoire" (*Representations* 26, 1989). Archives as mnemonic infrastructure; press as crystallisation of contemporary memory (eclass.uoa.gr PDF of Nora 1989).
- **Assmann, Jan.** *Das kulturelle Gedächtnis* (1992; Eng. *Cultural Memory and Early Civilization*, 2011). Distinction communicative vs cultural memory; relevant to how a fascist-era daily transitions from "current discourse" to canonised heritage.
- **Halbwachs, Maurice.** *La mémoire collective* (1950). Genealogical antecedent for both Nora and Assmann.

### 5. Newspapers as historical sources
- **Schudson, Michael.** *Discovering the News* (1978). Classic on the social construction of "news."
- **AHA, "Late to the Feast: Newspapers as Historical Sources"** (*Perspectives*, 1993). Diagnoses historians' traditional distrust of the press.
- **Vella, Stephen.** "Newspapers," in M. Dobson and B. Ziemann (eds), *Reading Primary Sources* (2009). Practical methodology (bias, layout, agenda-setting).

### 6. Italian fascism / 1943 / Il Messaggero
- **De Felice, Renzo.** *Mussolini l'alleato, 1940-1945* (2 vols, 1990, 1996), esp. vol. I tomo 2 covering July-September 1943. Standard documentary reconstruction of the *crollo* and Salò (Wikipedia, "Renzo De Felice").
- **Gentile, Emilio.** *Il culto del littorio* (1993; *The Sacralization of Politics in Fascist Italy*, 1996); *La via italiana al totalitarismo* (1995). Theory of fascism as political religion - directly explains the rhetorical register of *Il Messaggero* under the regime (Wikipedia, "Emilio Gentile").
- **Pavone, Claudio.** *Una guerra civile* (1991; *A Civil War*, 2013). Frames 1943-45 as triple war (patriotic / civil / class); essential for any month spanning the *crollo*.
- **Murialdi, Paolo.** *La stampa del regime fascista* (Laterza, 1986) and *Storia del giornalismo italiano* (Il Mulino, latest edn). Standard Italian-language history of the fascist press; covers MinCulPop press directives and *Il Messaggero* specifically.
- **Forno, Mauro.** *La stampa del Ventennio: strutture e trasformazioni nello stato totalitario* (Rubbettino, 2005). Structural account of fascist press control.

### 7. Digital history methodology
- **Cohen, Daniel J., and Roy Rosenzweig.** *Digital History: A Guide to Gathering, Preserving, and Presenting the Past on the Web* (2005). Foundational handbook.
- **Hitchcock, Tim.** "Confronting the Digital" (*Cultural and Social History*, 2013). Influential reflection on the methodological costs and gains of digitised search.

## Recent literature (2015-2024)

- **Guldi, Jo, and David Armitage.** *The History Manifesto* (Cambridge UP, 2014; OA). Argues for a return to *longue durée*, explicitly enabled by digital archives. Direct programmatic statement for what Mausoleo operationalises (Cambridge Core; Guldi, joguldi.com).
- **Romein, C. A., et al.** "State of the Field: Digital History" (*History*, 105/365, 2020). Synthesis review of digital-history methods incl. text mining, NER, network analysis (Wiley Online).
- **Blaxill, Luke.** *The War of Words: The Language of British Elections, 1880-1914* (Royal Historical Society / Boydell, 2020). First UK historical monograph using text mining as principal method - methodological precedent (Royal Historical Society blog, "The Trouble with Text Mining," 2023).
- **Ehrmann, Maud, et al. (impresso project).** "Transparent generosity. Introducing the impresso interface for the exploration of semantically enriched historical newspapers" (*Historical Methods*, 2024). The closest existing precedent for Mausoleo: hierarchical/semantic indexing across multilingual newspaper corpora; explicitly addresses scale and granularity (T&F).
- **Beals, M. H., and Emily Bell.** *The Atlas of Digitised Newspapers and Metadata* (2020). Reminder that computational claims depend on OCR quality and metadata provenance.
- **Salmi, Hannu.** *What is Digital History?* (Polity, 2020). Concise textbook framing.
- **Fridlund, Oiva, and Paju (eds).** *Digital Histories* (Helsinki UP, 2020; OA). Case studies on press analysis.
- **Tworek, Heidi.** *News from Germany* (Harvard UP, 2019). Newspapers as *infrastructure*, not just content.
- **Corner, Paul.** *The Fascist Party and Popular Opinion in Mussolini's Italy* (OUP, 2012; pb 2022). Uses unpublished archival material to show "real-existing fascism" was experienced negatively - reframes how we read regime-aligned papers like *Il Messaggero* (LSE Review of Books).
- **Albanese, Giulia, and Roberta Pergher (eds).** *In the Society of Fascists* (Palgrave, 2012, with later revs). Cultural history of fascism's everyday penetration; useful for reading mundane press content.

## Italian-specific sources (1943, Il Messaggero, fascist-era press)

- **Murialdi, Paolo.** *La stampa del regime fascista* (Laterza, 1986). Treats MinCulPop *veline* (press directives) shaping *Il Messaggero* and other dailies.
- **Cannistraro, Philip V.** *La fabbrica del consenso: fascismo e mass media* (Laterza, 1975). Foundational study of fascist media control.
- **Tranfaglia, Nicola.** *La stampa del regime, 1932-1943* (Bompiani, 2005). Period-specific.
- **Forno, Mauro.** *Informazione e potere. Storia del giornalismo italiano* (Laterza, 2012). Treatment of 1943.
- **De Felice, Renzo.** *Mussolini l'alleato* (1990, 1996). Reconstructs 25 July, the Badoglio 45 days, and the post-September RSI period.
- **Gentile, Emilio.** *Il culto del littorio* (1993); *Fascismo: storia e interpretazione* (Laterza, 2002). Symbolic vocabulary visible on every front page.
- **Pavone, Claudio.** *Una guerra civile* (1991). Essential if the chosen month is post-September.
- *Il Messaggero* under fascism was directed by figures including Virginio Gayda (1921-26); the *Tritone* offices were a focal point of crowd action on 25-26 July 1943 (Wikipedia, "Il Messaggero"; ns-taeter-italien.org, "Rome, 25 July 1943"). For citable academic detail use Murialdi and Forno.

## Key arguments to deploy

1. **Mausoleo as a Braudelian instrument.** The seven-level hierarchy materialises Braudel's tripartite time. Cite Braudel 1958 and Guldi/Armitage 2014 to legitimate the design choice as historiographically motivated, not merely UX-driven.

2. **Reconciling distant and close reading.** Following Moretti (2013) and impresso (Ehrmann et al. 2024), Mausoleo refuses the binary: distant reading (Year/Decade) and close reading (Paragraph) coexist; the unit-of-analysis is selectable rather than imposed.

3. **Microhistorical retrieval.** Ginzburg's evidential paradigm justifies paragraph-level granularity: a single anomalous detail (a casualty list, a censored ellipsis) can carry disproportionate interpretive weight. Mausoleo preserves that affordance where pure aggregation would destroy it.

4. **Critical press-source methodology.** Following Murialdi, Cannistraro, Forno, Vella, *Il Messaggero* 1943 is a regime-aligned source: computational summaries must be read *against* MinCulPop directives. This is a strength of the source - it indexes what the regime wanted readers to think.

5. **Archive as *lieu de mémoire*.** Following Nora and Assmann, Mausoleo participates in the transformation of the fascist daily press from "communicative" into "cultural" memory; the system is curation, not neutral access, and should be acknowledged reflexively.

## Suggested case-study questions / hypotheses (scope: one month in 1943)

1. **July 1943 - the *crollo* in real time.** How does *Il Messaggero* narrate 1-31 July 1943 (Allied landings in Sicily 10 July, bombing of Rome 19 July, Grand Council vote 24-25 July, Mussolini's dismissal, Badoglio government)? At what level of Mausoleo's hierarchy does the regime change become detectable - does a Day-level summary register the *crollo* as discontinuity, or does it require Article-level reading? Tests whether multi-scale automated summarisation detects a regime rupture.

2. **September 1943 - dual occupation and editorial pivot.** If the month is September (8 Sept armistice, German occupation, early RSI), how does lexicon, masthead, and topic distribution shift across 30 days? Month-level summary should make the pivot visible; paragraph-level retrieval should expose euphemisms ("alleati" -> "anglo-americani").

3. **A "normal" month - aggregating the everyday.** For a non-rupture month, can a coherent monthly summary be produced without losing event-level specificity? Addresses Ginzburg's worry about loss of the singular under aggregation; operationalises Moretti's claim that distant reading complements rather than replaces close reading.
